# 1. What is Phishing?

Phishing is a cyberattack in which attackers impersonate trusted individuals or organizations to trick victims into revealing sensitive information such as usernames, passwords, banking details, or personal data.

Phishing attacks are commonly carried out through:

- Emails
- SMS messages (Smishing)
- Phone calls (Vishing)
- Fake websites
- Social media messages

### Common Objectives

- Steal login credentials
- Obtain financial information
- Install malware
- Commit identity theft

---

# 2. Types of Phishing

## Email Phishing

The most common phishing technique where attackers send fake emails that appear to come from trusted organizations.

**Example:**

An email claiming your bank account has been locked and asking you to click a fake login link.

---

## Spear Phishing

A targeted phishing attack aimed at a specific individual or organization.

Attackers personalize emails using publicly available information.

---

## Whaling

Targets high-profile individuals such as CEOs, executives, or government officials.

The goal is usually financial fraud or access to confidential information.

---

## Smishing

Phishing conducted through SMS text messages.

Example:

"Your package delivery failed. Click here to reschedule."

---

## Vishing

Phishing performed through voice calls.

Attackers pretend to be bank representatives, technical support, or government officials.

---

## Clone Phishing

A legitimate email is copied, and malicious links or attachments replace the original ones before being resent to victims.

---

# 3. Detection Techniques

Phishing attacks can often be identified by observing warning signs such as:

- Suspicious sender email addresses
- Spelling and grammatical mistakes
- Urgent or threatening language
- Unexpected attachments
- Fake login pages
- Mismatched URLs
- Requests for passwords or sensitive information

Always verify website URLs before entering login credentials.

---

# 4. Prevention Methods

Effective measures to prevent phishing attacks include:

- Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)
- Verify sender identities
- Avoid clicking unknown links
- Use updated antivirus software
- Keep operating systems and browsers updated
- Use email spam filters
- Verify website URLs before logging in
- Never share passwords via email or phone

Organizations should also deploy email authentication technologies such as:

- SPF
- DKIM
- DMARC

---

# 5. Real-World Phishing Incidents

## Google and Facebook Invoice Scam (2013–2015)

Attackers impersonated a legitimate hardware supplier and sent fake invoices to Google and Facebook.

Approximately **$100 million** was fraudulently transferred before the scam was discovered.

---

## Twitter Bitcoin Scam (2020)

Attackers gained access to Twitter's internal systems using social engineering techniques.

Verified accounts belonging to major public figures were used to promote a cryptocurrency scam.

---

## Colonial Pipeline Attack (2021)

Although primarily a ransomware attack, investigators believe compromised credentials obtained through phishing-related techniques contributed to the breach.

The incident disrupted fuel distribution across the United States.

---

# 6. User Awareness Recommendations

Users can significantly reduce phishing risks by following these practices:

- Think before clicking links.
- Verify website addresses carefully.
- Enable Multi-Factor Authentication.
- Use strong, unique passwords.
- Keep software updated.
- Report suspicious emails immediately.
- Avoid downloading unexpected attachments.
- Use a password manager.
- Never share passwords or OTPs.

Regular cybersecurity awareness training is recommended for organizations.

---

# Conclusion

Phishing remains one of the most common and effective cyber threats. Attackers continuously develop new techniques to deceive users into revealing sensitive information. Combining technical security measures with user awareness, strong authentication methods, and safe browsing habits greatly reduces the risk of successful phishing attacks.
